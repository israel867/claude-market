## Email 2 · Day 2

**Subject line A:** So is ceramic coating even worth $800?
**Subject line B:** The quiz made you skeptical. Good. Now read this.

**Preview text:** If half the claims are BS, why would anyone pay for this?

---

After taking the BS Detector, most people go through the same two stages.

Stage one: "I knew it. I knew those claims were inflated."

Stage two hits about two days later: "Wait — if the marketing is mostly hype, is ceramic coating even worth the money?"

You might be in stage two right now. Fair. Let me give you a straight answer.

Yes. But not for the reasons you've been sold.

It's not a force field. A bird will still crap on your car. A shopping cart will still scratch it. Rain spots will still show up. If that's what you're paying to prevent, save your money.

Here's what you're actually paying for.

Your car stays cleaner between washes. Dirt, pollen, road film — they sit on top of the coating instead of grinding into your clear coat. When you wash, everything comes off easy. No scrubbing. No swirl marks from fighting stuck-on grime.

Your paint holds up longer. UV rays break down clear coat over time. That's what makes paint look faded and chalky after a few years in the Texas sun. A coating slows that process down. Significantly. Your paint keeps its depth and color years longer than it would unprotected.

You stop waxing. No more reapplying sealant every three months. No weekend spent polishing. You wash it, you dry it, it looks like it just rolled out of the shop. Not because of magic — because the surface is doing what a surface is supposed to do.

That's worth $800 to $1,600 for most people who plan to keep their car 3+ years.

It's not worth it if you're expecting a miracle. And I'd rather tell you that now than take your money and watch you get disappointed in six months.

— Israel

## Sequence Notes

**Platform:** Any email tool (Mailchimp, ConvertKit, MailerLite). Set as automation triggered by quiz completion.

**A/B testing:** Test subject lines for 24 hours on a 20% sample, then send winner to remaining list.

**Rules:**
1. No urgency tactics. No "limited spots." No countdown timers. Ever.
2. Emails 1 and 2 have zero CTAs. Pure value. The only CTA in the entire sequence is Email 3.
3. Every email works standalone — if they only open one, they still got something useful.
4. Sign off as Israel every time. Never "The Kurtz Team." This is a person, not a brand.
5. Subject lines should feel like a text, not a marketing email.
6. Preview text must add new information — never repeat the subject line.
